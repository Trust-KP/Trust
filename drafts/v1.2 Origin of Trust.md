# 9 ORIGIN OF TRUST 

## 9.1	Core Principle

As social coordination expands beyond small relational communities, observers rarely possess direct knowledge of the full contribution history underlying an agent’s behaviour. 

In closely connected communities, trust often arises from remembered interaction and shared experience. Members observe each other’s actions across time—for example, a carpenter whose work has repeatedly been seen and evaluated—so allowing cooperation can occur without additional verification mechanisms.

When interaction extends beyond familiar communities, observers may rely on identity markers that compress elements of contribution history into portable signals. Titles, lineage, institutional affiliations, and recognised social roles allow others to infer that an individual has been validated within a particular social or cultural context.

In large and impersonal environments, however, neither direct familiarity nor identity recognition is sufficient to sustain cooperation. Procedural validation systems therefore emerge as a minimal mechanism for coordination. Contracts, certification processes, regulatory oversight, escrow systems, and other rule-based structures allow agents with no prior relationship to cooperate under shared procedural guarantees. 

These mechanisms reduce the amount of personal trust required by transferring part of the verification process from individual judgement to recognised institutional procedures.

Trust therefore enables a baseline level of cooperation even when interpersonal familiarity and identity-based recognition are absent. While stronger forms of trust may later emerge through observed performance or repeated interaction, procedural validation provides the minimum structure necessary for strangers to coordinate behaviour within complex societies.

Contribution history nevertheless remains the underlying evidential substrate from which trust judgements emerge. Actions performed within the system generate observable effects that accumulate across time, producing signals that allow observers to infer reliability, credibility, and the likely continuation of behaviour.

Trust is unidirectional in accumulation and bidirectional in interpretation.

## 9.2	Signal and Observation 

Contribution events occurring within a system are not directly accessible to all observers. Instead, these events produce observable traces that may be perceived, recorded, or transmitted within social and institutional environments. These observable traces are referred to as signals.

Signals may arise through direct observation of behaviour, communication between agents, institutional records, reputational transmission, or symbolic markers associated with past actions. Because observers rarely possess complete knowledge of the underlying contribution history, signals provide partial evidence from which expectations about future behaviour are formed.

Signals therefore function as the informational bridge between underlying contribution events and the interpretation of trust. Observers interpret these signals using valuation principles in order to form expectations regarding the likely continuation of behaviour under conditions of uncertainty.

### 9.2.1	Signal delay

Trust, credibility, and credit respond to observed signals rather than directly to underlying contribution events. When signals are delayed, hidden, or absent, observers may continue operating with outdated credibility assessments despite changes in the underlying contribution reality.

### 9.2.2	Signal Circulation

Signals derived from contribution history may circulate across relational, symbolic, procedural, and instrumental domains as they move through social and institutional systems. While these signals may be interpreted through different trust lenses depending on context, the underlying accumulation of contribution remains anchored in contribution history.

In many situations, observers cannot directly observe or reconstruct the full sequence of contribution events that produced the underlying history. As a result, signals originating in one informational domain may be translated or represented within another. For example, instrumental performance records may be validated through procedural evaluation, procedural validation may be communicated through symbolic markers, and symbolic recognition may influence relational perception within interpersonal contexts.

This circulation does not alter the underlying contribution history itself. Instead, it reflects the movement and reinterpretation of signals across different informational structures as observers attempt to interpret limited evidence.

This relationship may be summarised as a structural principle:

Trust is unidirectional in accumulation but bidirectional in interpretation.

Contribution events accumulate irreversibly over time as part of contribution history. By contrast, the signals used to interpret trust may move across multiple interpretive domains depending on the information available to observers and the valuation principles applied during decision-making.

## 9.3	Valuation Principle

Signals derived from contribution history do not themselves produce trust judgements.

Observers must interpret these signals according to a valuation principle that determines what counts as relevant evidence of reliability and how different signals should be weighted when forming expectations about future behaviour.

A valuation principle therefore defines the interpretive rule through which signals are assessed under conditions of uncertainty. Different observers, institutions, and cultural environments may apply different valuation principles even when interpreting the same underlying signals.

Within this framework, valuation principles operate through the interpretive lenses described in the following section. Each lens evaluates signals according to a distinct informational logic that determines how evidence of contribution history is recognised and interpreted.

Trust formation therefore arises not from signals alone, but from the application of valuation principles to signals derived from contribution history.

Trust formation requires that contribution signals be interpretable within the receiving system's credibility framework.

Agents whose contribution signals fall outside the interpretive structure of a cooperative system may remain untrusted even when their underlying contribution reliability is high.

## 9.4	Interpretive Lenses of Trust

The framework distinguishes four primary interpretive lenses through which observers interpret signals derived from contribution history under conditions of uncertainty.

These lenses do not represent separate “types” or “substances” of trust. Instead, they represent distinct interpretive mechanisms applied to the same underlying contribution history.

Observers rarely have direct access to the full contribution history of another agent. Instead, they encounter observable signals—such as behaviour, outcomes, institutional records, reputational indicators, or identity markers—that reflect aspects of that history. Each lens interprets these signals according to different informational rules.

The lenses therefore represent alternative mechanisms through which observers infer reliability from contribution-relevant signals.

Each lens applies a distinct interpretive rule to the same set of observed signals. In practice, multiple lenses may operate simultaneously, and their interpretations may either reinforce or contradict one another depending on the institutional context.

Human observers typically integrate signals across several lenses simultaneously when forming trust judgements. Individuals may combine relational familiarity, symbolic identity, procedural legitimacy, and observable performance when interpreting the behaviour of others.

Institutional systems, by contrast, often rely on a single dominant interpretive lens within a given decision process. Institutions require stable and reproducible rules in order to evaluate signals across large populations. As a result, institutional trust mechanisms frequently compress interpretation into a primary lens—for example regulatory validation, formal certification, reputational signalling, or measurable performance outcomes.

This distinction reflects the different functional requirements of human judgement and institutional coordination. Human interpretation allows flexible integration of multiple informational cues, whereas institutional systems simplify interpretation in order to achieve scalable and consistent decision processes.

These lenses do not correspond one-to-one with definitions in any single academic discipline. They represent a descriptive synthesis intended to clarify distinctions that are often implicit, overlapping, or conflated in existing literature.

Structurally, the four lenses can be organised along two underlying dimensions frequently observed in institutional practice.

The first dimension concerns the basis of interpretation, ranging from personal foundations (rooted in relationships or identity) to impersonal foundations (rooted in rules or recorded evidence).

The second dimension concerns the source of legitimacy, ranging from normative or identity-based justification to operational or performance-based justification.

When interpreted through these dimensions, the four primary trust lenses emerge:

Lens		Basis		Legitimacy
Coordination	personal	operational
Identity	personal	normative
Structural	impersonal	normative
Capability	impersonal	operational

These lenses therefore represent four distinct ways of interpreting contribution-relevant signals rather than four separate forms of trust.

Which lens dominates in a given situation depends on the institutional structure, governance arrangements, and cultural expectations of the domain.

The framework distinguishes these lenses descriptively without privileging any single lens as universally superior.

The interpretive lenses described in this section operate at the stage of trust formation. Later stages of the framework—such as credibility aggregation and credit deployment—do not introduce additional interpretive lenses but instead operate on the trust judgements produced through these interpretive mechanisms.

### 9.4.1	Coordination lens

In many social systems, the coordination lens functions as the earliest seed of trust formation. Before formal institutions, symbolic identities, or procedural verification mechanisms develop, agents rely primarily on direct experience of coordinated interaction to assess the reliability of others. 

Observed behaviours such as responsiveness, mutual adjustment, and successful joint action provide the earliest signals from which trust interpretations emerge.

As societies expand and interactions occur across greater social distance, direct coordination becomes increasingly difficult for individuals to observe and remember. Institutional mechanisms therefore emerge to extend the reach of trust interpretation. 

Symbolic reputation compresses historical recognition into portable signals, procedural validation establishes rule-based verification of behaviour, and capability measurement records observable performance outcomes. 

These mechanisms allow trust signals to scale beyond the limits of direct interpersonal experience while preserving the underlying coordination logic from which trust initially develops.

### 9.4.2	Identity lens
  
The Identity lens interprets signals derived from contribution history through shared cultural meaning, identity markers, and socially recognised symbols.

Within this lens, observers infer reliability or legitimacy from signs, narratives, or roles that carry recognised meaning within a shared interpretive framework. Signals therefore arise from the cultural significance attached to these symbols rather than from direct observation of contribution events.
  
Such signals may include titles, institutional affiliations, professional identities, traditions, or cultural narratives. Observers interpret these markers as indicators that an agent has been recognised or validated within a particular community or institutional context.

Identity interpretation may be socially durable even when its informational basis becomes partially detached from direct evidence of performance or capability. Because identity meaning is culturally constructed, identity signals may persist even when the original evidentiary foundations weaken or disappear.

In many cases, identity markers originate from earlier processes of validation, such as procedural certification or demonstrated capability. Over time these markers may become culturally stabilised symbols that circulate independently of their original evidentiary context.
  
The Identity lens therefore compresses wider histories of validation or reputation into portable markers that can be recognised across social distance, even when the underlying contribution history is not directly observed.

Examples include professional titles, institutional prestige, religious roles, national symbols, and cultural traditions.

Procedural validation reduces the amount of personal trust required for coordination by transferring evaluation responsibility from individual observers to recognised institutional processes.

### 9.4.3	Structural lens
  
The Structural lens interprets signals derived from contribution history through adherence to recognised processes, rules, and governance structures.
  
Within this lens, observers rely on the integrity of defined procedures rather than on direct familiarity with the individual agent. Credibility is inferred when an agent successfully passes through recognised institutional processes designed to evaluate behaviour, competence, or eligibility.

Structural interpretation therefore functions as a rule-bound validation mechanism. Observers rely on the outcome of recognised processes as proxies for deeper evaluation of contribution history.
  
In this context, confidence is placed in the legitimacy and consistency of the governing procedures rather than in the personal characteristics of individual participants.

A defining feature of the Legitimacy lens is that coordination may continue even when participants do not personally trust one another, provided that the governing procedures are perceived as legitimate and enforceable.

This lens enables cooperation across large populations by replacing personal familiarity with institutional validation mechanisms.

Procedural interpretation is common in institutional environments such as courts, regulatory systems, examinations, certification processes, and bureaucratic administration.

### 9.4.4	Capability lens

The Capability lens interprets signals derived from contribution history through observable performance and recorded evidence of behaviour.

Within this lens, observers evaluate credibility retrospectively using measurable signals such as outcomes, performance metrics, or documented records of contribution events. Judgements are therefore expressed as comparative or predictive assessments rather than as interpersonal evaluations.

Such assessments may take the form of scores, rankings, probabilities, or other quantitative indicators that summarise observable signals derived from an agent’s contribution history.

Capability interpretation enables coordination across large institutional systems by allowing decisions to be made based on recorded performance data rather than direct personal familiarity.

This interpretive mechanism is widely used in markets, financial systems, institutional governance, professional evaluation, and large-scale organisational coordination.

Unlike interpersonal interpretation, the instrumental lens does not depend on relational continuity, moral obligation, or emotional connection. Instead, it provides a structured method for interpreting recorded signals of behaviour under conditions of uncertainty.

In principle, if contribution histories were fully observable and reliably recorded, instrumental interpretation would provide the most precise representation of continuation-relevant behaviour. In practice, however, contribution histories remain partial, bounded, and mediated through institutional observation systems.

## 9.5	Institutional Trust Infrastructure

In large cooperative systems, individual observers cannot independently interpret all signals derived from contribution history. Societies therefore develop institutional structures that perform validation and signal interpretation on behalf of participants.

These institutions function as trust-bearing structures. Through procedures such as certification, licensing, regulation, and record keeping, institutions evaluate contribution signals and produce validated indicators that can be recognised by other participants.

By transferring the burden of signal interpretation from individuals to recognised institutional processes, societies reduce the amount of interpersonal trust required for coordination across large populations.

Institutional trust systems therefore enable cooperation between agents who lack direct familiarity with one another. The reliability of these systems depends on the perceived legitimacy and consistency of the governing procedures through which signals are validated and transmitted.

## 9.6 Trust Lens Architecture Across Individual and Institutional Systems

The four trust lenses described in this framework operate across two interpretive environments: individual interaction and institutional coordination. Although the interpretive lenses themselves remain structurally identical, the informational environments in which signals are generated and interpreted differ between these two contexts.

Trust interpretation always occurs through the judgement of observers. Observers evaluate signals generated from the contribution history of trust bearers. Trust bearers may be individuals or organisations whose behaviour produces observable outcomes within cooperative systems.

Institutional environments do not interpret trust independently. Instead, they structure how signals are generated, verified, recorded, and transmitted between participants. Through procedures such as documentation, auditing, certification, and standardised reporting, institutions stabilise the signal environment in which observers apply the four trust lenses.

As a result, trust interpretation within institutional settings reflects the evaluation of organisational contribution histories within structured signal environments, whereas interpersonal contexts rely more heavily on direct observation and informal signal interpretation.

### 9.6.1	Trust Interpretation - Individual

At the individual level, agents interpret signals derived from contribution history through personal cognitive and social judgement. Individuals may integrate signals across multiple lenses simultaneously when forming expectations about the behaviour of others.

Interpersonal interpretation arises from remembered interaction history, familiarity, and perceived stability of relationships.

Identity interpretation relies on identity markers, reputational cues, narratives, and socially recognised roles that signal recognition within a shared community or cultural framework.

Structural interpretation evaluates whether behaviour conforms to recognised rules, procedures, or norms that define acceptable participation within a system.

Capability interpretation assesses observable outcomes, recorded performance, and perceived competence derived from evidence of prior behaviour.

Because human judgement integrates multiple informational cues, these interpretive lenses may operate simultaneously and may shift dynamically depending on the context of interaction.

### 9.6.2	Trust Interpretation - Institutional

At the institutional level, organisations coordinate behaviour through structured systems that interpret signals derived from contribution history. These systems allow large populations of agents to cooperate without requiring direct interpersonal familiarity.

Interpersonal interpretation within institutional environments arises through repeated interactions between individual agents representing their respective organisations. Familiarity and perceived reliability develop through these professional relationships even when the broader institutional participants remain organisational entities.

Identity interpretation appears through institutional identity markers such as brand reputation, recognised affiliations, titles, and other socially recognised indicators that compress broader histories of recognition into portable signals.

Legitimacy interpretation appears through formal verification mechanisms including contracts, regulatory compliance, certification processes, audits, and rule-based governance structures that validate behaviour according to recognised institutional procedures.

Capability interpretation appears through measurable performance indicators such as operational records, financial results, statistical analysis, and other documented evidence of outcomes.

Institutional systems tend to rely more heavily on legitimacy and capability interpretation because large-scale coordination requires stable and reproducible evidence structures.

Institutional validation reduces the informational burden on individual observers by transferring the evaluation of contribution signals from personal judgement to recognised procedures.

Trust ultimately resides in the judgement of individual agents. Institutions do not hold trust themselves but instead stabilise and transmit signals that influence trust interpretation across participants.

## 9.7	Relationship Between the Two Levels

Individual and institutional interpretations coexist within the same cooperative environment but operate under different informational constraints.

Individuals interpret signals derived from contribution history through integrated judgement across multiple trust lenses. Human evaluation therefore combines interpersonal experience, identity recognition, perceived legitimacy of behaviour, and observed capability.

Institutions, by contrast, stabilise interpretation through structured evaluation systems designed to support coordination across large populations. These systems emphasise legitimacy and capability evidence because large-scale coordination requires reproducible and verifiable signals.

As a result, the same contribution signals may produce different trust interpretations depending on whether the observer is an individual agent or whether the signals are interpreted through institutional systems.

## 9.8	Interaction Contexts

Trust interpretation occurs within specific interaction contexts determined by the types of agents involved in the relationship. 

Within the Trust Framework, agents may be either:
-	Individuals, or 
-	institutions and cooperation 

Therefore, arises across four possible interaction contexts: 
-	Individual–Individual interactions, 
-	Individual–Institution interactions, 
-	Institution–Individual interactions, and 
-	Institution–Institution interactions. 

Each context operates under different informational constraints and behavioural expectations. Individual–Individual interactions typically rely more heavily on Interpersonal and symbolic interpretation, while Institution–Institution interactions tend to depend more on procedural and instrumental evidence structures. 

Mixed contexts between individuals and institutions often combine personal judgement with institutional verification systems. 

These interaction contexts do not create new forms of trust but define the environments within which contribution signals are interpreted through the existing trust lenses.

## 9.9	Information Representation Across Trust Modes

Different trust modes rely on different levels and forms of information in order to establish credibility.
Interpersonal trust relies primarily on unrecorded interaction memory and operates with minimal formal information. Observers interpret signals derived from lived interaction, familiarity, and shared experience. Trust arises from accumulated interpersonal knowledge within a small social network, where individuals rely on remembered behaviour rather than formal documentation or verification.
Symbolic trust relies on recognised identity signals that compress wider histories of validation into culturally meaningful markers. These may include titles, institutional affiliation, certifications, or other symbolic indicators that communicate recognised status within a community or institutional system. Trust judgements in this mode depend on the social meaning attached to these signals rather than on direct evaluation of recorded performance evidence.
Procedural trust depends on observable adherence to established processes or rules. In such contexts, credibility is established through signals generated by recognised evaluation procedures such as examinations, licensing processes, regulatory approval, or formal compliance systems. Observers rely on the legitimacy and consistency of the governing procedures rather than on direct familiarity with the agent or interpretation of symbolic identity signals alone.
Instrumental trust relies on detailed recorded signals derived from contribution history. Credibility is evaluated retrospectively using measurable evidence such as outcomes, performance metrics, financial records, or other documented indicators of behaviour. This form of trust therefore presupposes institutional mechanisms capable of recording, verifying, and interpreting contribution-relevant signals across time
As institutional systems expand and coordination occurs across increasing social distance, the informational requirements for establishing trust correspondingly increase. Instrumental trust represents the most information-intensive form because it relies on the systematic interpretation of recorded contribution signals rather than on interpersonal familiarity, symbolic recognition, or procedural validation alone.
Different trust modes therefore differ not in the existence of contribution history, but in how much of that history can be observed, compressed, validated, or recorded through different informational mechanisms.

## 9.10	Coexistence Rule

The coexistence rule applies to the operational use of trust modes within social and institutional systems. 
It does not imply that the modes share identical informational foundations.
Across the framework, trust signals ultimately arise from the interpretation of contribution history under conditions of uncertainty. 
The different trust modes represent alternative interpretive mechanisms applied to this underlying contribution history when information is incomplete, socially compressed, institutionally validated, or formally recorded.
Accordingly, the modes coexist at the level of interpretation, while the accumulation of contribution history remains the underlying informational substrate from which trust signals are derived.
Institutions are not a distinct trust mode. They are structured environments in which relational, instrumental, procedural, and symbolic trust coexist and interact.
Trust modes coexist within social and institutional systems because different actors, decisions, and contexts may rely on different interpretive mechanisms. However, at the point of a specific trust judgement, a dominant interpretive mode typically governs how contribution history is evaluated.
Accordingly, the modes coexist at the systemic level but remain functionally distinct at the level of decision. Each mode interprets the same underlying contribution history through a different informational structure. The accumulation of contribution history therefore forms the shared informational substrate, while the trust modes represent alternative lenses through which that history may be interpreted under different informational conditions.
4.13	Transformation and Loss Across Trust Modes
The coexistence rule describes how different trust modes operate simultaneously within social and institutional systems.
Although the modes rely on the same underlying contribution history, they do not share identical informational foundations. Instead, each mode interprets signals derived from contribution history using a distinct informational structure.
Accordingly, the modes coexist at the level of interpretation, while the accumulation of contribution history remains the underlying informational substrate from which trust-relevant signals arise.
Institutions are not themselves a distinct trust mode. Rather, they provide structured environments within which relational, symbolic, procedural, and instrumental interpretations may operate together.
Different actors, decisions, and contexts may rely on different interpretive mechanisms when forming trust judgements. Within complex systems, multiple trust signals may therefore circulate simultaneously across relational familiarity, symbolic identity, procedural validation, and instrumental performance evidence.
However, at the point of a specific trust judgement, one interpretive lens typically dominates how the available signals are evaluated. The coexistence rule therefore operates across the system as a whole, while individual decisions usually rely on a primary interpretive mechanism.
The trust modes therefore coexist at the systemic level but remain functionally distinct at the level of decision. Each mode interprets signals derived from the same underlying contribution history through a different informational structure.
Contribution history thus forms the shared informational substrate of trust systems, while the trust modes represent alternative interpretive lenses through which that history becomes observable and actionable under conditions of uncertainty.

## 9.11	Cross-Lens Interpretation

Observers may interpret the same signal through different trust lenses depending on the context of evaluation. A signal that originates within one informational domain may therefore acquire different meanings when interpreted through another lens.
For example, symbolic signals of authority may be interpreted as evidence of performance capability, procedural compliance may be treated as moral legitimacy, and measurable performance indicators may be interpreted as indicators of relational trustworthiness. These shifts do not represent failures of any particular interpretive lens. Instead, they reflect changes in how observers evaluate available signals.
Because multiple interpretive lenses coexist within social and institutional systems, observers may apply different interpretive rules to the same signal when forming trust judgements in different contexts.

### 9.11.1	Interpretive Scope and Update Mechanisms

Trust, credibility, and credit assessments depend on the interpretation of signals derived from contribution history. Because observers do not possess complete knowledge of underlying events, these assessments rely on interpretive frameworks that determine which signals are considered relevant and how those signals are evaluated.
The valuation principle defines the interpretive scope of this process. It determines which signals fall within the domain of evaluation and how different signals are weighted when forming trust, credibility, or credit assessments. Different observers, institutions, or contexts may apply different valuation principles, leading to variations in interpretation even when the same signals are observed.
Interpretation may also evolve over time through three mechanisms:
Data change occurs when new signals become available or previously unavailable information is revealed. Additional evidence may strengthen, weaken, or contradict earlier assessments.
Parameter change occurs when observers adjust the relative weighting assigned to signals already within the interpretive scope. Changes in institutional priorities, governance standards, or contextual conditions may alter how strongly particular signals influence evaluation.
Model change occurs when the interpretive framework itself changes. Observers may adopt different interpretive rules, governance structures, or evaluation models when interpreting signals.
Through these mechanisms, assessments of trust, credibility, and credit may change even though the underlying contribution history remains fixed.

## 9.12	Relationship to Other parts

This part of the framework is purely descriptive.
It establishes the conceptual distinctions, interpretive lenses, and informational structures through which trust signals are interpreted under conditions of uncertainty.
It does not introduce formal modelling, optimisation, or operational decision rules.
Any later part of the framework that:
• models trust accumulation
• introduces ledgers, state variables, or quantitative representations
• defines decision thresholds or governance rules
• analyses systemic stability, resilience, or collapse
operates within a separate formal domain and must declare that shift in epistemic scope explicitly.
The purpose of this separation is to prevent descriptive distinctions about trust interpretation from being conflated with formal modelling frameworks or institutional decision systems.
Later parts of the document examine how trust signals evolve through time within social and institutional systems.
This includes the processes through which trust signals are created from contribution events, transformed through circulation across interpretive modes, and subsequently reinterpreted, reinforced, weakened, or reorganised as new information becomes available.
These later sections therefore address the dynamic behaviour of trust systems, while Part I remains concerned only with the descriptive structure through which trust signals are interpreted.
Part I describes the interpretive structure through which trust becomes observable.
Subsequent parts analyse how these signals may be formalised, modelled, or operationalised within specific institutional contexts.
4.16	Observer Perspective and Temporal Absence
Trust, credibility within this framework are interpreted from the perspective of agents who observe and interpret signals under conditions of incomplete information.
Observers rarely have direct access to the full sequence of contribution events that occur within the system. Instead, they encounter signals generated by those events through observation, communication, institutional records, or social transmission. These signals are interpreted in real time even though the underlying contribution events may be spatially or temporally distant from the observer.
As a result, trust and credibility judgements operate under conditions of temporal absence. Observers form expectations about the reliability of others without directly observing the behaviour that will occur at the moment of future interaction. Trust therefore functions as an interpretive mechanism that allows agents to coordinate action despite the absence of direct observation of future behaviour.
Signals originating from past contribution events propagate through social and institutional systems, allowing observers to form expectations about the continuation of behaviour even when the relevant actions cannot be directly observed. Trust and credibility judgements therefore reflect the interpretation of transmitted signals rather than direct access to the underlying events themselves.
Within this framework, trust and credibility should therefore be understood as observer-dependent interpretations of transmitted signals, rather than intrinsic properties of agents or actions.
